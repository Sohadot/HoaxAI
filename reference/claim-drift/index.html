Claim Drift

Claim Drift must exist because the claim a reader encounters is rarely the claim a source originally supported. Compression, amplification, attribution shifts, certainty inflation, context stripping, translation loss, and model-summary rewriting each change what the evidence layer can support—often without any deceptive intent.

Definition

Claim Drift is the Hoax.ai term for structural change in what a claim asserts—or appears to assert—as it moves through extraction, republication, headline writing, translation, screenshotting, model summarization, institutional reporting, and policy citation.

Drift is an analytical object, not a moral verdict. A drifted claim may be more certain, more accusatory, more general, or more specific than its source basis supports. Drift may be accidental, procedural, or structural. Drift is not deception by default.

Within the evidence-posture system, Claim Drift explains why claim–source traceability and evidence chain discipline are necessary. The claim at the end of circulation is an evidence object with its own posture—not a transparent window to an original truth.

Claim Drift Chain

The Claim Drift Chain is Hoax.ai's staged model for examining how claims transform across circulation layers. Reviewers map where drift begins and what each stage can still support.

  1. Original claim — The claim as stated in the earliest governed source record available for review, with scope and qualifiers intact.
  2. Source framing — How the publishing source titles, introduces, or contextualizes the claim, including editorial framing that narrows or widens implication.
  3. Extracted claim — The claim after pull-quotes, clips, tables, or data extracts separate it from surrounding qualification language.
  4. Reposted claim — The claim as re-published by secondary distributors, aggregators, or accounts without full chain context.
  5. Compressed claim — The claim after headline compression, character limits, or summary boxes remove nuance and scope constraints.
  6. Expanded claim — The claim after commentary, thread replies, or analysis layers add implications not present in source text.
  7. Interpreted claim — The claim after analysts, moderators, or models infer meaning beyond literal assertion.
  8. Unsupported claim — The claim as it exists when traceability to source basis fails or when certainty exceeds available evidence.

Chains are rarely linear in the wild, but the model gives reviewers a reproducible vocabulary for identifying where unsupported certainty entered the stream.

Drift Types

Hoax.ai distinguishes drift patterns because each pattern creates different evidence-posture obligations and different failure modes for institutions.

  • Compression drift — Qualifiers, scope limits, and uncertainty markers disappear while the core assertion survives in sharper form.
  • Amplification drift — Reach and repetition make a narrow claim feel broadly established without adding evidential support.
  • Attribution drift — The claim becomes attached to different subjects, sources, or events than the original basis supported.
  • Certainty drift — Modal language shifts from possibility to fact, from allegation to established event, without new chain evidence.
  • Context drift — Situational framing changes so the same words imply different actions, timelines, or responsibilities.
  • Translation drift — Cross-language movement alters nuance, legal meaning, or technical specificity in ways reviewers may not notice.
  • Model-summary drift — Automated summaries add entities, causal links, or confident paraphrases not present in source artifacts.
  • Headline drift — Headline compression replaces qualified source language with flat assertions optimized for circulation.
  • Citation drift — Secondary citations paraphrase or misattribute source claims while retaining citation appearance.

Naming drift types helps teams avoid a common category error: treating any change as proof of intentional deception. Drift analysis is evidence discipline, not accusation infrastructure. It supports responsible evidence interpretation and synthetic media evidence review without implying a public detector or scoring system.

Category Thesis

Hoax.ai cannot own evidence posture if it tracks artifacts but ignores how claims mutate in the wild. Claim Drift makes circulation legible as an evidence problem, not only a distribution problem.

Without drift vocabulary, institutions debug provenance while publishing drifted claims. They strengthen hash chains while headlines invent certainty. Claim Drift aligns posture work with how humans and models actually encounter assertions.

Why This Term Is Necessary

Existing language focuses on true/false adjudication or on source reputation, not on claim transformation across layers. "Out of context" is intuitive but too vague for governance, audit, or synthetic-media review workflows.

AI-era circulation adds model-summary drift at scale. A claim may be born drifted—produced already overconfident relative to artifact support. Serious evidence systems need a term that covers human and machine transformation alike.

Evidence System Relationships

Claim Drift connects tightly to Claim–Source Traceability because drift is what happens when traceability weakens or when readers ignore traceability signals. It connects to Evidence Chain because each chain link can introduce drift; chain continuity is not claim stability.

Drift interacts with Context Collapse when stripped frames accelerate compression and certainty drift. It interacts with Output Boundary when published language must reflect the claim stage being described, not an imagined original. Artifact–Subject Separation blocks drifted claims from becoming subject verdicts.

Evidence Posture must be assigned to the claim as encountered, not to an idealized source claim reviewers wish still existed. Attribution Boundary blocks drifted accusations from becoming subject verdicts. Interpretation Risk rises when drift is unrecognized.

Failure Modes

  • Headline verdicts: Compression drift turns qualified findings into flat accusations published as fact.
  • Traceability theater: Teams link to a source while publishing a drifted claim the source does not support.
  • Model laundering: Model-summary drift introduces confident false specifics that humans treat as analyst findings.
  • Policy citation drift: Institutional reports cite earlier reports, amplifying certainty while chain quality decays.
  • Deception default: Any drift is framed as intentional manipulation, destroying proportionality and audit trust.

Boundary Logic

Claim Drift enforces disciplined separation: claim movement is not deception unless separate evidence supports deceptive intent. Artifact condition is not subject judgment even when drifted claims name subjects. Evidence limitation is not a truth verdict when reviewers document that only a compressed claim is available for posture review. Interpretation risk is not accusation when teams warn that drift makes overreading likely.

Institutional Relevance

Journalists, researchers, analysts, platforms, trust and safety teams, legal reviewers, and AI system designers all operate on drifted claims daily. Claim Drift gives them shared vocabulary to slow publication, demand chain repair, or mark outputs as unsupported without defaulting to fake/real binaries.

Compliance and evidence review workflows benefit when drift stages are logged: teams can show why a headline overstates a filing, why a model summary added a subject reference, or why a repost cannot support enforcement language. Synthetic-media governance programs need drift analysis because synthetic artifacts often enter circulation already detached from creation context.

Practical Reading Frame

Consider a fictional internal memo stating that a file "shows signs consistent with synthetic generation under limited review conditions." A screenshot caption reposts: "Company admits fake video." No real party is named in this teaching example.

Claim Drift analysis maps compression and certainty drift between memo claim and caption claim. Traceability review asks whether the caption can link to supported language in the memo. Posture language describes the caption claim as high-risk unsupported assertion—not as proof that any person deceived anyone.

What It Cannot Decide

Claim Drift does not prove a claim true, does not prove deception, does not certify a source, and does not assign guilt. It does not replace primary source review when primary sources are available.

When Claims Must Be Treated as Altered, Limited, or Not Assessable

Claim Drift supplies operational rules for Evidence Posture Standard v1 claim handling:

  • Altered: When compression, headline, translation, or model-summary drift changes claim scope or certainty, reviewers must treat the circulated claim as a distinct claim object—not as transparent access to the original.
  • Limited: When traceability partially holds but qualifiers are stripped, output language must restore limitation markers and refuse unsupported certainty.
  • Not assessable: When drift severs claim–source traceability and chain prerequisites fail, responsible classification may stop without implying the claim is false.

Drift may be accidental, procedural, or structural. It does not prove deception, manipulation, or motive by default. Claim Drift protects Artifact–Subject Separation by keeping analysis on claim movement, not subject accusation.

Standard-Readiness

How Claim Drift contributes to Evidence Posture Standard v1

Evidence Posture Standard v1 must treat claims as objects that change across transmission—not as stable truth proxies. Claim Drift provides the claim-movement discipline the standard requires.

The standard can require drift-stage logging: original claim, extracted claim, reposted claim, compressed claim, interpreted claim. Each stage carries its own posture obligations. Claim–Source Traceability becomes enforceable when drift vocabulary names where traceability broke.

When drift is present, the standard can prohibit publishing downstream claims as if they were source-certified. This protects institutions from headline verdicts and model-summary laundering without requiring fake/real adjudication.

Allowed and Prohibited Output Language

Allowed output language

  • Naming drift type: compression drift, certainty drift, attribution drift, context drift, translation drift, model-summary drift, headline drift, citation drift.
  • Describing the claim as encountered at a specific chain stage.
  • Requiring traceability repair before escalation.
  • Marking drifted claims as limited or not assessable when support fails.
  • Documenting accidental or procedural drift without intent inference.

Prohibited output language

  • Treating drift as proof of deception, manipulation, or motive.
  • Publishing compressed or headline claims as source-equivalent.
  • Assigning guilt because a claim changed across reposts.
  • Certifying source truth from drifted downstream assertions.
  • Fake/real verdict language substituting for drift analysis.

Claim Drift does not prove deception, does not prove manipulation, and does not prove motive. It describes structural claim movement that may be accidental, procedural, or structural—not bad faith by default.

Standard Relationship
