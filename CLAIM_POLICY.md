# Hoax.ai Claim Policy

## Purpose

This policy governs the claims Hoax.ai may make about its system, content, tools, outputs, and public positioning.

The asset must avoid overclaiming.

Its strategic value depends on trust, restraint, and evidence discipline.

## Approved Core Claim

Hoax.ai may claim:

> Hoax.ai is a governed evidence-risk intelligence system for the synthetic media age.

This means the asset is designed to classify evidence posture, synthetic risk signals, source confidence, contextual gaps, and deception patterns.

## Approved Claim Types

Hoax.ai may state that it:

- examines evidence posture;
- classifies synthetic risk signals;
- evaluates source confidence;
- identifies provenance gaps;
- maps contextual uncertainty;
- explains deception-pattern categories;
- provides bounded evidence-risk outputs;
- supports trust-and-safety workflows;
- builds reference infrastructure around AI-era evidence risk;
- and helps users understand what must be examined before belief, action, publication, or escalation.

## Prohibited Claims

Hoax.ai must not claim that it:

- proves truth;
- detects all fake content;
- detects all deepfakes;
- certifies authenticity;
- guarantees verification;
- determines legal truth;
- makes final judgments about people or institutions;
- replaces journalists, investigators, courts, legal teams, or forensic experts;
- or provides absolute certainty.

## Restricted Claim Terms

The following terms require caution:

- "verified";
- "certified";
- "authentic";
- "fraud";
- "lie";
- "hoax confirmed";
- "truth score";
- "fake score";
- "detection guarantee";
- "forensic proof".

These terms may only be used in explanatory or comparative contexts, not as claims of Hoax.ai output authority.

## Preferred Output Language

Use:

- Evidence Posture;
- Source Confidence;
- Synthetic Risk;
- Contextual Risk;
- Provenance Gap;
- Verification Gap;
- Deception Pattern;
- Further Review Required.

Do not use:

- True;
- False;
- Fake;
- Real;
- Guilty;
- Fraudulent;
- Confirmed Hoax.

## Public Trust Rule

Every public claim must pass this test:

1. Does it avoid final truth verdicts?
2. Does it communicate uncertainty honestly?
3. Does it protect against reputational accusation?
4. Does it reflect evidence posture rather than belief?
5. Does it strengthen long-term trust in the asset?

If a claim fails any of these tests, it must not be published.

## Governing Sentence

**A framework, not a verdict.**
